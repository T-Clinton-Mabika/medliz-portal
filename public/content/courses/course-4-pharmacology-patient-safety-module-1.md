### Pharmacokinetics vs Pharmacodynamics

These two disciplines describe how the body handles drugs and how drugs affect the body respectively.

**Pharmacokinetics (PK)** — What the body does to the drug:

- **Absorption**: How a drug enters the bloodstream (affected by route, formulation, and GI motility).
- **Distribution**: How a drug spreads through body compartments (influenced by protein binding and lipid solubility).
- **Metabolism**: How the body chemically modifies the drug (primarily hepatic, via cytochrome P450 enzymes).
- **Excretion**: How the drug or its metabolites leave the body (primarily renal).

**Pharmacodynamics (PD)** — What the drug does to the body:

- **Mechanism of action**: How the drug produces its effect at the molecular level (receptor agonism, enzyme inhibition, ion channel blockade, etc.).
- **Dose-response relationship**: The relationship between drug concentration and clinical effect.
- **Therapeutic index**: The ratio between the toxic dose and the effective dose — a narrow therapeutic index demands careful monitoring (e.g., warfarin, digoxin, lithium).

### Drug Classes & Mechanisms

Common drug classes encountered in clinical practice:

| Drug Class | Mechanism | Example |
|------------|-----------|---------|
| Beta-blockers | Block β-adrenergic receptors | Metoprolol |
| ACE inhibitors | Inhibit angiotensin-converting enzyme | Ramipril |
| SSRIs | Block serotonin reuptake transporter | Sertraline |
| Statins | Inhibit HMG-CoA reductase | Atorvastatin |
| Opioids | Agonise μ-opioid receptors | Morphine |
| Penicillins | Inhibit bacterial cell wall synthesis | Amoxicillin |

### Routes of Administration

The route of administration affects the speed, completeness, and predictability of drug action:

1. **Oral (PO)**: Convenient but subject to first-pass metabolism.
2. **Intravenous (IV)**: Immediate onset, 100% bioavailability, but requires clinical monitoring.
3. **Intramuscular (IM)**: Good absorption; useful when IV access is unavailable.
4. **Subcutaneous (SC)**: Slower absorption; used for insulin, heparin, and biologics.
5. **Topical/transdermal**: Avoids first-pass effect; useful for localised or sustained action.
6. **Inhaled**: Rapid onset; targets pulmonary tissue (e.g., bronchodilators).

### The Prescribing Cascade

The prescribing cascade occurs when a side effect of one drug is misidentified as a new clinical condition and treated with an additional drug — which may itself cause further side effects. This is a common source of polypharmacy and medication harm in older patients.

> Example: An NSAID causes elevated blood pressure → a new antihypertensive is prescribed → the antihypertensive causes ankle oedema → a diuretic is added.

Regularly reviewing all prescribed medications against current clinical need is an essential prescribing skill.

### Summary

A strong grounding in pharmacology enables safer prescribing, more effective medication review, and better patient counselling. Understanding PK/PD principles allows clinicians to anticipate drug behaviour and tailor therapy to individual patient characteristics.
