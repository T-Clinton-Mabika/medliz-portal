Medication errors are among the most frequent types of preventable harm in healthcare. This module examines the systems, behaviours, and habits that underpin safe prescribing practice.

### The Scale of Medication Error

Medication errors occur at every stage of the medicines management process — prescribing, transcription, dispensing, administration, and monitoring. Globally, medication-related harm costs billions in additional care and results in significant patient morbidity and mortality.

Key error categories include:

- **Wrong drug** (including look-alike/sound-alike errors)
- **Wrong dose** (including ten-fold errors in paediatric prescribing)
- **Wrong route** (e.g., intrathecal vincristine — a catastrophic and preventable error)
- **Wrong patient**
- **Wrong frequency or duration**
- **Omission errors** (failure to prescribe or administer)

---

### The Swiss Cheese Model

The Swiss Cheese Model (Reason, 1990) conceptualises accident causation as multiple layers of defence, each with "holes" representing weaknesses. Errors only cause harm when holes align across all layers — when system-level defences are insufficient to catch human error.

This model supports a systems thinking approach to medication safety: rather than blaming individuals, effective safety culture investigates the system failures that allowed the error to occur.

### Safe Prescribing Principles

The WHO recommends a structured approach to prescribing using the **P-drug method**:

1. **Define the patient's problem**
2. **Specify the therapeutic objective**
3. **Verify suitability of your P-drug** for this patient
4. **Start the treatment**: write the prescription correctly
5. **Give information, instructions, and warnings** to the patient
6. **Monitor and stop** the treatment when appropriate

Additional safe prescribing habits:

- Always check allergies before prescribing
- Use generic names rather than brand names to reduce confusion
- Avoid verbal orders wherever possible; if necessary, use read-back verification
- Apply weight-based calculations for paediatric and high-risk adult dosing
- Review the full medication list for interactions before adding a new drug

### High-Alert Medications

Certain drug classes carry a disproportionate risk of serious harm if used in error. The Institute for Safe Medication Practices (ISMP) designates these as **high-alert medications**:

- Anticoagulants (especially heparin, warfarin, direct oral anticoagulants)
- Insulin (all formulations)
- Opioids (all routes)
- Concentrated electrolytes (potassium chloride injection, hypertonic saline)
- Cytotoxic chemotherapy agents
- Neuromuscular blocking agents

Institutions typically apply additional safeguards for these agents, including double-checking, restricted storage, and enhanced labelling.

---

### Medication Reconciliation

Medication reconciliation is the process of creating a complete and accurate list of a patient's medications at every care transition (admission, transfer, discharge). It is one of the highest-yield patient safety interventions and significantly reduces discrepancy-related harm.

Best practice elements include:

- Gathering medicines information from multiple sources (patient, carer, GP, pharmacy records)
- Documenting rationale for any intentional changes
- Providing a clear medicines summary on discharge

---

### Summary

Safe prescribing is a disciplined, systematic process. By internalising safe prescribing principles, remaining vigilant for high-risk scenarios, and supporting systems-level safety culture, clinicians can significantly reduce the burden of preventable medication harm.
