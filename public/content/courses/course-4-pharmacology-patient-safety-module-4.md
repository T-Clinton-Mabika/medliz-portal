Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is one of the greatest threats to global health. This module explores the principles of antibiotic stewardship and the prescriber's critical role in preserving the efficacy of existing antimicrobial agents.

### The AMR Crisis

Antimicrobial resistance develops when microorganisms evolve mechanisms to withstand the drugs designed to kill them. The WHO has designated AMR as one of the top global public health threats, with projections suggesting that drug-resistant infections could cause 10 million deaths annually by 2050 if no action is taken.

Key drivers of AMR include:

- Overuse and inappropriate prescribing of antibiotics in human medicine
- Widespread use in agriculture and animal husbandry
- Poor infection prevention and control in healthcare settings
- Insufficient development of new antimicrobial agents
- Inadequate surveillance and diagnostic infrastructure in lower-income settings

---

### Principles of Antibiotic Stewardship

Antibiotic stewardship programmes (ASPs) aim to optimise antimicrobial prescribing to improve patient outcomes while reducing unnecessary exposure. Core principles include:

1. **Right drug**: Select the most appropriate agent based on likely pathogen and local resistance patterns.
2. **Right dose**: Ensure adequate concentration at the site of infection.
3. **Right route**: Use oral therapy when clinically appropriate (IV-to-oral switch).
4. **Right duration**: Avoid unnecessarily prolonged courses.
5. **Right patient**: Confirm infection is truly present and bacterial in aetiology before prescribing.

---

### Common Resistance Mechanisms

Understanding how bacteria resist antibiotics informs rational prescribing:

| Mechanism | Description | Example Organism |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Beta-lactamase production** | Enzyme degrades the antibiotic | ESBL-producing E. coli |
| **Efflux pumps** | Drug is actively pumped out of the cell | Pseudomonas aeruginosa |
| **Target modification** | Drug can no longer bind its target | MRSA (altered PBP2a) |
| **Reduced permeability** | Drug cannot enter the cell | Gram-negative outer membrane changes |
| **Enzymatic inactivation** | Drug chemically modified and inactivated | Aminoglycoside resistance |

### De-escalation & IV-to-Oral Switch

Two of the highest-impact stewardship interventions are:

**De-escalation**: Once culture and sensitivity results are available, narrow the antimicrobial spectrum to the most targeted effective agent. Broad-spectrum empirical therapy should not be maintained indefinitely.

**IV-to-oral switch (IVOS)**: Evidence demonstrates that many infections can be treated effectively with oral antibiotics. Switching from IV to oral therapy earlier reduces line-associated infection risk, shortens hospital stay, and reduces antibiotic selection pressure.

---

### The Prescriber's Responsibilities

Every prescriber plays a role in stewardship. Key responsibilities include:

- Sending appropriate microbiological specimens **before** starting antibiotics wherever possible.
- Documenting the indication, intended dose, route, and duration at the time of prescribing.
- Reviewing antibiotic prescriptions at 48–72 hours with culture results.
- Resisting patient pressure to prescribe antibiotics for viral illnesses.
- Consulting microbiologists or infectious disease specialists for complex cases.

### Summary

Responsible antibiotic prescribing is not merely a clinical skill — it is an ethical obligation to future patients who will depend on effective antimicrobial agents. By applying stewardship principles consistently, every prescriber contributes to the preservation of one of medicine's most vital resources.
