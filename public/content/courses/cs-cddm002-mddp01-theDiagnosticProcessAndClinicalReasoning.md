Effective diagnosis is both an art and a science. This module explores the structured approaches clinicians use to move from presenting symptoms to accurate diagnosis, while minimising error and bias.

### The Diagnostic Process

Diagnosis is a systematic process of gathering, interpreting, and synthesising clinical information. It typically follows this sequence:

1. **History Taking**: Eliciting the patient's story — onset, duration, character, associated symptoms, and relevant medical background.
2. **Physical Examination**: Observing and assessing the patient to identify signs.
3. **Investigations**: Ordering and interpreting tests (blood work, imaging, biopsies).
4. **Differential Diagnosis**: Generating a ranked list of possible diagnoses.
5. **Working Diagnosis**: Selecting the most probable diagnosis to guide management.

---

### Cognitive Biases in Diagnosis

Clinicians are susceptible to reasoning errors. Awareness of common biases is the first step toward mitigating them.

| Bias | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Anchoring** | Fixating on an initial impression | Diagnosing chest pain as musculoskeletal without ruling out cardiac causes |
| **Availability** | Overweighting recently seen diagnoses | Over-diagnosing a rare condition seen recently |
| **Premature closure** | Stopping the diagnostic process too early | Accepting one diagnosis without considering alternatives |
| **Framing effect** | Being influenced by how information is presented | A referral letter bias affecting clinical objectivity |

### Clinical Decision Support

Modern practitioners have access to a range of tools to support diagnostic accuracy:

- **Clinical guidelines** from bodies such as NICE, WHO, and specialist societies.
- **Decision aids** and risk stratification tools (e.g., Wells score for DVT, CURB-65 for pneumonia).
- **Electronic health record alerts** flagging abnormal values or drug interactions.
- **Multidisciplinary team (MDT) meetings** enabling collaborative review of complex cases.

### Uncertainty in Medicine

Diagnosis often involves uncertainty. A key skill is calibrating confidence appropriately — neither over-committing to a diagnosis prematurely nor failing to act pending perfect information. Communicating uncertainty clearly with patients and colleagues is an essential component of safe practice.

---

### Red Flag Symptoms

Certain symptoms require urgent escalation regardless of the broader clinical picture. Common red flags across specialties include:

- Unexplained weight loss
- Haemoptysis (coughing up blood)
- Severe or sudden-onset headache
- Unexplained altered consciousness
- Signs of sepsis (fever, tachycardia, hypotension, confusion)

Always document the presence or absence of red flags in your clinical notes.

---

### Summary

Accurate diagnosis requires a methodical approach, self-awareness regarding cognitive bias, and a willingness to revise your assessment in light of new evidence. These skills develop through deliberate practice and reflective learning throughout your clinical career.
