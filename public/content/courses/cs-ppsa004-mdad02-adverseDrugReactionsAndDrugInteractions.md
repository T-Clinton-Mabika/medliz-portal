Adverse drug reactions (ADRs) are a leading cause of preventable patient harm. This module provides a structured approach to recognising, classifying, and managing ADRs, as well as understanding the mechanisms behind clinically significant drug interactions.

### Classifying Adverse Drug Reactions

The Rawlins-Thompson classification (Types A–F) is the most widely used system:

| Type | Name | Characteristic | Example |
|------|------|---------------|---------|
| **A** | Augmented | Dose-dependent, predictable | Hypoglycaemia with insulin |
| **B** | Bizarre | Dose-independent, unpredictable | Anaphylaxis with penicillin |
| **C** | Chronic | Related to cumulative dose | Adrenal suppression with long-term steroids |
| **D** | Delayed | Occurs long after exposure | Drug-induced malignancy after chemotherapy |
| **E** | End-of-use | Occurs on withdrawal | Benzodiazepine withdrawal syndrome |
| **F** | Failure | Unexpected lack of efficacy | Contraceptive pill failure due to enzyme-inducing drugs |

Type A reactions account for approximately 80% of all ADRs and are, in principle, preventable.

### Recognising ADRs in Clinical Practice

Common presentations of ADRs include:

- Skin rashes, urticaria, angioedema
- GI disturbance (nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea)
- Electrolyte abnormalities (e.g., hypokalaemia with diuretics)
- Hepatotoxicity (elevated transaminases)
- Nephrotoxicity (rising creatinine)
- CNS effects (confusion, sedation, tremor)
- Cardiac arrhythmias (QT prolongation with certain antibiotics and antipsychotics)

A high index of suspicion is required. Any new symptom in a patient on medication should prompt consideration of an ADR.

### Mechanisms of Drug Interactions

Drug-drug interactions (DDIs) occur via pharmacokinetic or pharmacodynamic mechanisms:

**Pharmacokinetic interactions:**

- **Enzyme induction**: One drug accelerates the metabolism of another (e.g., rifampicin reduces warfarin efficacy).
- **Enzyme inhibition**: One drug slows the metabolism of another, increasing toxicity risk (e.g., clarithromycin elevates simvastatin levels, causing myopathy).
- **Protein binding displacement**: Less clinically significant than once thought; usually transient.

**Pharmacodynamic interactions:**

- **Additive effects**: Two drugs with the same mechanism (e.g., two CNS depressants causing excessive sedation).
- **Synergistic effects**: The combined effect exceeds the sum of individual effects.
- **Antagonistic effects**: One drug opposes the action of another (e.g., beta-blockers reversing salbutamol bronchodilation in asthma).

### High-Risk Drug Combinations to Know

The following combinations require particular vigilance:

- **Warfarin + NSAIDs**: Increased bleeding risk
- **ACE inhibitor + potassium-sparing diuretic**: Hyperkalaemia
- **Methotrexate + trimethoprim**: Bone marrow suppression
- **Serotonergic drugs combined** (e.g., SSRI + tramadol): Serotonin syndrome
- **QT-prolonging drugs** (e.g., antipsychotics + certain antibiotics): Torsades de pointes

---

### Reporting ADRs

Healthcare professionals are expected to report suspected ADRs to national pharmacovigilance systems (e.g., the Yellow Card scheme in the UK, MedWatch in the USA). Reporting is especially important for:

- New drugs (Black Triangle/▼ designation in the UK)
- Serious ADRs regardless of drug age
- Unexpected reactions

Under-reporting remains a significant problem, limiting the ability of regulatory agencies to detect emerging signals.

---

### Summary

ADR recognition requires a systematic approach and a low threshold for suspicion. Understanding interaction mechanisms allows clinicians to anticipate risk and make informed prescribing decisions. Reporting ADRs is both a professional obligation and a contribution to wider patient safety.
