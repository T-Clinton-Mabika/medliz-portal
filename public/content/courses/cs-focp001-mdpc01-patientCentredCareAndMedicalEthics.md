### Why Patient-Centred Care Matters

Patient-centred care places the individual — their needs, preferences, and values — at the heart of every clinical decision. Research consistently shows that patients who feel heard and respected are more likely to engage with treatment plans, report higher satisfaction, and achieve better clinical outcomes.

Key elements of patient-centred care include:

1. **Respecting individuality**: Recognising that each patient brings a unique background, culture, and set of concerns.
2. **Shared decision-making**: Involving patients as active partners in decisions about their care.
3. **Coordination and continuity**: Ensuring smooth handovers and consistent communication across care teams.
4. **Emotional support**: Acknowledging the psychological impact of illness alongside its physical dimensions.
5. **Access to information**: Providing clear, honest, and timely communication about diagnosis, treatment options, and prognosis.

### The Four Pillars of Medical Ethics

Medical ethics provides the moral framework within which clinical decisions are made. The four pillars — first articulated by Beauchamp and Childress — remain the cornerstone of bioethical reasoning worldwide.

![Ethics Diagram](https://picsum.photos/seed/ethics/600/300)
*Figure 2: The four pillars of medical ethics: Autonomy, Beneficence, Non-maleficence, and Justice.*

#### 1. Autonomy
Autonomy is the patient's right to make informed decisions about their own care. Respecting autonomy requires:

- Providing patients with all relevant information in a form they can understand.
- Confirming that the patient has the mental capacity to make the decision.
- Accepting a patient's right to refuse treatment, even if you disagree with their choice.
- Obtaining valid informed consent before any procedure or intervention.

> **Capacity** is decision-specific and time-specific. A patient may have capacity to consent to one procedure but not another, or may have capacity today but not tomorrow.

#### 2. Beneficence
Beneficence is the obligation to act in the patient's best interest. It requires clinicians to:

- Apply evidence-based practice to maximise therapeutic benefit.
- Weigh the likely benefits of an intervention against its risks and burdens.
- Consider the patient's own definition of "benefit" — which may differ from a purely clinical perspective.

#### 3. Non-maleficence
Often summarised as *primum non nocere* ("first, do no harm"), non-maleficence requires clinicians to avoid causing unnecessary harm. In practice, this means:

- Prescribing treatments only when the benefit justifies the risk.
- Monitoring patients for adverse effects and responding promptly.
- Recognising the limits of your own competence and seeking support when needed.

#### 4. Justice
Justice demands that healthcare resources and burdens are distributed fairly. This includes:

- Treating all patients with equal respect regardless of background, identity, or social circumstances.
- Advocating for patients who face systemic barriers to care.
- Using healthcare resources responsibly, considering the needs of the wider population.

### Communication in Clinical Practice

Clear, compassionate communication is one of the most powerful clinical tools available. Poor communication is a leading cause of medical error, patient dissatisfaction, and complaints.

#### The Calgary-Cambridge Model

The Calgary-Cambridge framework provides a structured approach to the clinical consultation, encompassing five phases:

1. **Initiating the session**: Establishing rapport and identifying the reason for the consultation.
2. **Gathering information**: Exploring the patient's symptoms, ideas, concerns, and expectations (ICE).
3. **Physical examination**: Conducting the examination with appropriate explanation and consent.
4. **Explanation and planning**: Sharing findings and agreeing on a management plan.
5. **Closing the session**: Summarising, safety-netting, and confirming understanding.

#### Active Listening

Active listening is a skill that must be cultivated deliberately. It involves:

- Maintaining appropriate eye contact and open body language.
- Allowing the patient to complete their initial statement without interruption.
- Using verbal and non-verbal cues to demonstrate engagement.
- Reflecting and summarising to confirm understanding.
- Tolerating silence — patients often need a moment to formulate their thoughts.

#### Breaking Bad News: The SPIKES Protocol

Delivering difficult information requires a structured, empathetic approach. The SPIKES protocol is widely used:

| Step | Component | Key Action |
|------|-----------|------------|
| **S** | Setting | Ensure a private, comfortable environment; have tissues available |
| **P** | Perception | Assess what the patient already knows or suspects |
| **I** | Invitation | Gauge how much information the patient wants right now |
| **K** | Knowledge | Deliver the information clearly and without jargon |
| **E** | Emotions | Respond to emotional reactions with empathy and silence |
| **S** | Strategy & Summary | Agree on next steps and confirm understanding |

### Professional Boundaries & the Therapeutic Relationship

The therapeutic relationship is built on trust, but it must be carefully maintained within appropriate professional boundaries. Boundaries protect both the patient and the practitioner.

Key principles include:

- Maintaining professional behaviour at all times, including in written and digital communications.
- Never pursuing personal relationships with current or recent patients.
- Being transparent about your role and the limits of your practice.
- Recognising when personal feelings or biases risk affecting clinical objectivity, and seeking supervision.

---

### Empathy vs. Sympathy

These terms are often confused but have distinct meanings in clinical practice:

- **Sympathy** involves sharing or feeling the same emotion as the patient — "I feel sad too."
- **Empathy** involves understanding and acknowledging the patient's emotional experience without losing your own perspective — "I can see this news is devastating for you."

Empathy is generally more therapeutically valuable than sympathy. It validates the patient's experience while preserving the clinician's ability to think and act clearly on their behalf.

### Vulnerable Patients & Special Considerations

Certain patient groups require additional consideration to ensure equitable, safe care:

- **Children and young people**: Involve appropriately, consider Gillick competence for consent in under-16s.
- **Patients with cognitive impairment**: Use capacity assessment frameworks and involve appropriate decision-makers.
- **Patients with communication barriers**: Use professional interpreters — never rely on family members for clinical interpreting.
- **Patients from marginalised communities**: Be alert to health inequalities and ensure inclusivity in communication and care planning.

---

### Maintaining Professional Standards

Every clinical interaction is governed by professional codes of conduct. In the UK, the General Medical Council's *Good Medical Practice* outlines the duties of a doctor, including:

- Making the care of the patient your first concern.
- Being honest and open, and acting with integrity.
- Working collaboratively with colleagues.
- Maintaining your skills and knowledge through ongoing professional development.

---

### Summary

Patient care begins and ends with people. By grounding your practice in the four ethical pillars, developing strong communication skills, and maintaining professional standards, you lay the foundation for a career defined by trust, compassion, and clinical excellence.
