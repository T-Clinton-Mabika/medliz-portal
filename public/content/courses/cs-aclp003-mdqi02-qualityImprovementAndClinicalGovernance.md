Quality improvement (QI) is a systematic approach to enhancing healthcare outcomes and processes. This module explores the frameworks, methodologies, and leadership responsibilities that underpin effective clinical governance.

### What Is Clinical Governance?

Clinical governance is the framework through which healthcare organisations are accountable for continuously improving the quality of their services and safeguarding high standards of care. It encompasses:

- **Clinical audit** — measuring current practice against defined standards
- **Risk management** — identifying and mitigating potential harms
- **Education and training** — ensuring ongoing professional development
- **Evidence-based practice** — aligning clinical decisions with current research
- **Patient and public involvement** — incorporating the patient voice in service design

---

### The Plan-Do-Study-Act (PDSA) Cycle

The PDSA cycle is the most widely used QI methodology in healthcare. It supports iterative, evidence-based improvement:

1. **Plan**: Identify the problem, set a measurable aim, and design a change.
2. **Do**: Implement the change on a small scale.
3. **Study**: Collect and analyse data to assess the impact.
4. **Act**: Scale up successful changes; abandon or redesign ineffective ones.

Multiple rapid PDSA cycles accelerate learning and reduce the risk of large-scale failed implementations.

### Measuring What Matters

Effective QI requires robust measurement. Metrics typically fall into three categories:

| Metric Type | Definition | Example |
|-------------|------------|---------|
| **Process** | How care is delivered | % of patients receiving VTE prophylaxis within 12 hours of admission |
| **Outcome** | The result of care | 30-day readmission rate |
| **Balancing** | Unintended consequences of change | Did the new pathway reduce one complication while increasing another? |

Avoid the trap of measuring what is easy rather than what is meaningful.

### Root Cause Analysis (RCA)

When significant adverse events occur, RCA provides a structured methodology for understanding contributing factors without defaulting to individual blame. The process involves:

1. Establishing a timeline of events
2. Identifying contributing factors (human, system, environmental)
3. Using techniques such as the **5 Whys** to surface root causes
4. Generating actionable recommendations

### The Role of Senior Clinicians in QI

Advanced practitioners have specific responsibilities within clinical governance frameworks:

- **Championing a culture of openness**: Encouraging reporting of near-misses and adverse events.
- **Leading audit cycles**: Designing, implementing, and feeding back on clinical audits.
- **Engaging with mortality and morbidity (M&M) reviews**: Using these forums constructively.
- **Influencing organisational strategy**: Contributing to governance committees and policy development.

---

### Summary

Quality improvement is not a bureaucratic obligation — it is a professional and ethical imperative. Senior clinicians who engage deeply with QI methodology and governance structures have the greatest capacity to drive meaningful, sustained improvements in patient care.
