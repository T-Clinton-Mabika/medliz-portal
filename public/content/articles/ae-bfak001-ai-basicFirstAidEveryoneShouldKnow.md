Emergencies rarely announce themselves. A child chokes at the dinner table. A colleague collapses in the office. A stranger on the street clutches their chest. In those first critical minutes — before an ambulance arrives — the person standing closest determines the outcome. That person could be you.

This guide walks through the essential first aid skills every person should know, understand, and feel confident enough to use.

---

## 1. Before You Act — The Golden Rule

Before you touch anyone, ask one question: **Is it safe?**

Your safety comes first. Rushing into an unsafe scene turns one casualty into two. Scan for traffic, gas leaks, live electricity, or unstable structures. Eliminate hazards where you safely can, then approach.

Once safe, follow the **DR ABC** framework:

| Step | Meaning | What to do |
|------|---------|------------|
| **D** | Danger | Confirm scene is safe |
| **R** | Response | Tap shoulders, call their name |
| **A** | Airway | Head-tilt, chin-lift to open airway |
| **B** | Breathing | Look, listen, feel — up to 10 seconds |
| **C** | Circulation | If not breathing normally, start CPR |

Every first aid action should serve one of three goals: **preserve life, prevent deterioration, promote recovery.**

## 2. CPR — The Skill That Saves Lives

Cardiac arrest can happen to anyone, anywhere. CPR — Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation — keeps oxygenated blood moving to the brain until professional help arrives. For every minute without CPR, survival chances drop by roughly 10%.

![A first responder demonstrating chest compressions on a CPR training mannequin](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1516574187841-cb9cc2ca948b?w=800)
*Photo by Irwan on Unsplash — CPR training demonstration on a mannequin*

### How to Perform CPR on an Adult

1. Call emergency services immediately (or send someone while you begin).
2. Place the heel of one hand on the **centre of the chest**; interlock the other hand on top.
3. Compress **at least 5 cm deep** at **100–120 beats per minute** — the rhythm of *Stayin' Alive* by the Bee Gees is exactly right.
4. After every 30 compressions, give **2 rescue breaths** (tilt head, lift chin, seal your mouth over theirs, and breathe until you see the chest rise).
5. If you are untrained or uncomfortable with rescue breaths, **hands-only CPR** is still highly effective — just keep compressing.
6. Continue until an AED arrives, the person recovers, or EMS takes over.

**For children (1–8 years):** Use one hand; compress one-third of chest depth.  
**For infants:** Use two fingers only.

## 3. Using an AED

Automated External Defibrillators are now found in airports, shopping centres, gyms, and schools — and they are designed to be used by anyone, with no medical training required.

![A yellow AED device mounted on a public wall with clear signage](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1655947717064-53d8ac7f5861?w=800)
*Photo by Giulia May on Unsplash — A publicly mounted AED device*

1. Power on and follow the voice prompts — the machine guides every step.
2. Attach the pads to a bare, dry chest as shown on the diagrams.
3. Shout **"Clear!"** and ensure no one is touching the patient.
4. Deliver the shock if advised.
5. Resume CPR immediately for 2 minutes, then allow the AED to re-analyse.

> **You cannot make the patient worse.** The AED will only shock a heart that needs it.

## 4. Choking — Recognise and Respond

**Mild choking** (patient can cough, speak, or breathe): Encourage them to keep coughing. Do not interfere.

**Severe choking** (cannot cough, speak, or breathe):

- **Adults and children over 1:** Give 5 firm back blows between the shoulder blades, then 5 abdominal thrusts (Heimlich manoeuvre). Alternate until cleared or the person loses consciousness.
- **Infants under 1 year:** 5 back blows face-down over your forearm, then 5 chest thrusts. **Never perform abdominal thrusts on an infant.**
- If the person becomes unconscious: begin CPR and check the mouth for any visible object before each rescue breath.

## 5. Bleeding Control

Uncontrolled bleeding is one of the most preventable causes of death in trauma situations. Acting quickly and decisively makes all the difference.

- Apply **firm direct pressure** with a clean cloth or dressing and maintain it — do not keep lifting to check.
- **Elevate** the injured limb above heart level where possible.
- If blood soaks through, add more dressings on top. Do **not** remove the first layer — it is helping clot.
- For an **embedded object**, pack dressings around it and leave it in place. Removing it can cause catastrophic haemorrhage.

### Recognising Shock

Shock occurs when vital organs are starved of oxygenated blood. Signs include:
- Pale, cold, clammy skin
- Rapid, weak pulse
- Fast, shallow breathing
- Confusion or anxiety

**Treatment:** Lay the patient flat and raise legs approximately 30 cm (unless spinal, head, or chest injury is suspected). Keep them warm. Do not give food or water. Call emergency services immediately.

## 6. Burns

**The single most important action for a burn: cool it under cool running water for at least 10 minutes.** Do this immediately — it significantly reduces tissue damage.

![Running cool water over a burned hand at a sink](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1584308666744-24d5c474f2ae?w=800)
*Photo by Towfiqu Barbhuiya on Unsplash — Cooling a burn under running water*

**Do NOT use:** ice, butter, toothpaste, oil, or any home remedy. These cause further harm.

After cooling:
- Remove jewellery or clothing near the burn (unless stuck to skin).
- Cover loosely with cling film or a non-fluffy sterile dressing.
- Seek medical care for any burn larger than a postage stamp, or burns to the face, hands, feet, or genitals.

## 7. Stroke — Act FAST

Stroke is a medical emergency. Every second of delay causes further brain damage.

| Letter | Sign | What to look for |
|--------|------|-----------------|
| **F** | Face | Is one side drooping? |
| **A** | Arms | Can they raise both? |
| **S** | Speech | Is it slurred or confused? |
| **T** | Time | Call emergency services NOW |

Note the exact time symptoms began — this is critical information for medical staff. Do not give food or water.

---

## 8. The Recovery Position

When a person is **unconscious but breathing**, the recovery position prevents the tongue or vomit from blocking the airway.

1. Kneel beside the patient.
2. Place the near arm at a right angle to the body.
3. Bring the far arm across the chest, holding the back of the hand against the near cheek.
4. Pull the far knee up and roll them toward you onto their side.
5. Tilt the head back slightly to keep the airway open.
6. Monitor breathing continuously until help arrives.

## Final Word

First aid does not require perfection — it requires action. Doing something is almost always better than doing nothing. The skills in this guide can be learned in an afternoon and could save a life tomorrow.

*Consider attending a hands-on first aid course in your area. What you read becomes confidence only through practice.*
