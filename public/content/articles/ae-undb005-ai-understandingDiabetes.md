Diabetes is one of the fastest-growing chronic conditions in the world. Over 530 million adults are currently living with it — a number projected to reach 780 million by 2045. Yet despite its prevalence, diabetes remains widely misunderstood, underdiagnosed, and undertreated.

This article covers what diabetes actually is, how it differs across types, what the latest research has changed about prevention and management, and what living well with diabetes looks like in 2025.

---

## 1. What Is Diabetes?

Diabetes is a group of metabolic conditions characterised by chronically elevated blood glucose (blood sugar) levels. Glucose is the body's primary fuel source, and its movement from the bloodstream into cells depends on **insulin** — a hormone produced by beta cells in the pancreas.

When this system breaks down — either because the body produces no insulin, insufficient insulin, or responds poorly to it — glucose accumulates in the blood. Over time, this causes damage to blood vessels and nerves throughout the body, driving the serious complications that make diabetes a leading cause of blindness, kidney failure, heart disease, amputation, and premature death.

## 2. Type 1 Diabetes — An Autoimmune Condition

Type 1 diabetes (T1D) occurs when the immune system mistakenly destroys the pancreatic beta cells that produce insulin. Without insulin, cells cannot absorb glucose — a life-threatening situation.

T1D accounts for approximately 5–10% of all diabetes cases. It is most commonly diagnosed in children and young adults, though it can develop at any age. It is **not caused by diet or lifestyle** — it is an autoimmune disease with strong genetic components and likely environmental triggers (including certain viral infections).

People with T1D require **insulin for survival**, delivered via multiple daily injections or a continuous subcutaneous insulin pump.

### Advances in Type 1 Management

- **Continuous Glucose Monitors (CGMs)** — small sensors worn on the skin that measure interstitial glucose every few minutes, transmitting data to a smartphone in real time. CGM use has transformed glucose management and significantly reduced the burden of hypoglycaemia.
- **Closed-loop insulin delivery ("artificial pancreas")** — systems that combine a CGM with an insulin pump and an algorithm that automatically adjusts insulin delivery based on real-time glucose readings. Multiple systems are now approved and commercially available, representing the most significant advance in T1D management in decades.
- **Teplizumab** — the first disease-modifying therapy approved for T1D. This immune-modifying drug delays the onset of clinical T1D in high-risk individuals by approximately two years, representing a proof-of-concept that immune intervention can alter the disease course.

## 3. Type 2 Diabetes — A Metabolic Disease

Type 2 diabetes (T2D) accounts for around 90–95% of all diabetes cases. It develops when the body's cells become resistant to insulin (insulin resistance) and the pancreas can no longer compensate by producing sufficient additional insulin to maintain normal blood glucose.

T2D develops gradually over years — often preceded by a period of **prediabetes**, where glucose levels are elevated but not yet at diabetic thresholds. An estimated 374 million people worldwide have prediabetes, the majority unaware of it.

### Risk Factors

- Overweight and obesity (particularly central adiposity)
- Physical inactivity
- Family history of T2D
- Age over 45 (though T2D is increasingly diagnosed in younger adults and adolescents)
- History of gestational diabetes
- Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)
- Certain ethnicities (South Asian, African, Hispanic, and Indigenous populations face higher risk at lower BMI thresholds)

### Can Type 2 Diabetes Be Reversed?

The concept of T2D **remission** — achieving normal glucose levels without diabetes medication — is now supported by robust clinical evidence and formally recognised in major guidelines.

The landmark DiRECT trial demonstrated that intensive dietary intervention (a very low-calorie diet of approximately 800 kcal per day for 3–5 months, followed by structured weight maintenance) achieved remission in 46% of participants at one year and 36% at two years. Remission was strongly associated with the degree of weight loss.

Remission is not a cure — the underlying susceptibility remains, and remission requires ongoing maintenance. But it demonstrates that T2D is not inevitably progressive for all patients.

## 4. Gestational Diabetes

Gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM) develops during pregnancy in women who did not previously have diabetes. It occurs when pregnancy hormones cause insulin resistance that the pancreas cannot fully compensate for.

GDM affects approximately 14% of pregnancies globally and carries risks for both mother and baby — including large birth weight, preterm birth, and increased risk of caesarean delivery. Women with GDM have a 50% lifetime risk of developing T2D.

Management involves dietary modification, regular blood glucose monitoring, physical activity, and insulin therapy where glucose targets are not met. Blood glucose typically normalises after delivery, but testing at 6–12 weeks postpartum is essential.

## 5. Complications — Why Early Detection Matters

Chronically elevated blood glucose causes progressive damage throughout the body. The major complications include:

| Complication | Mechanism | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Diabetic retinopathy | Damage to retinal blood vessels | Leading cause of preventable blindness |
| Diabetic nephropathy | Glomerular damage | Leading cause of kidney failure requiring dialysis |
| Diabetic neuropathy | Peripheral nerve damage | Pain, numbness, foot ulcers, amputation risk |
| Cardiovascular disease | Accelerated atherosclerosis | Heart attack, stroke — leading cause of death in T2D |
| Diabetic foot disease | Combined vascular and neuropathic damage | Non-healing ulcers, infection, amputation |

The crucial insight is that most of these complications are **preventable** with early diagnosis and good glucose control. The UKPDS trial demonstrated decades ago that every 1% reduction in HbA1c (a measure of average glucose over 3 months) reduces microvascular complications by approximately 37% and reduces the risk of heart attack.

## 6. The Medication Revolution — New Drugs Changing Outcomes

The pharmacological management of T2D has been transformed over the past decade by two drug classes that do far more than lower blood glucose.

### SGLT2 Inhibitors (Gliflozins)

SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin, canagliflozin) lower blood glucose by causing the kidneys to excrete excess glucose in the urine. But their most significant benefits have proven to extend far beyond glucose control:

- Approximately **35–40% reduction in hospitalisation for heart failure**
- Significant **slowing of diabetic kidney disease progression**
- Modest **cardiovascular mortality reduction** in high-risk patients

They are now recommended as a priority add-on therapy for T2D patients with established cardiovascular disease, heart failure, or kidney disease.

### GLP-1 Receptor Agonists

GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide, dulaglutide) mimic a gut hormone that stimulates insulin secretion, suppresses glucagon, slows gastric emptying, and reduces appetite. They produce substantial weight loss — semaglutide (brand name Ozempic/Wegovy) has demonstrated average weight loss of 15–17% of body weight in trials, with some patients losing over 20%.

Beyond weight and glucose, high-dose semaglutide has demonstrated reductions in cardiovascular events, kidney disease progression, and — in 2024 trials — improvements in heart failure outcomes and liver disease. The SELECT trial demonstrated a 20% reduction in major cardiovascular events with semaglutide in obese patients without diabetes, expanding its clinical utility beyond T2D.

## 7. Living Well With Diabetes — Practical Pillars

Good diabetes management extends well beyond medication.

### Blood Glucose Monitoring

Understanding your glucose patterns is fundamental. CGMs have made this dramatically easier, providing continuous data rather than isolated snapshots. For people not using CGMs, structured self-monitoring — checking at strategic times rather than randomly — provides more actionable information.

### Diet

No single "diabetic diet" exists. The evidence supports several patterns:

- **Low-carbohydrate diets** produce the most rapid and dramatic short-term glucose improvements and can be highly effective for weight loss and medication reduction
- **Mediterranean diet** has the strongest long-term evidence for cardiovascular outcomes
- **Very low-calorie diets** are the most evidence-based approach for achieving remission in T2D

Across all patterns, reducing ultra-processed foods, added sugars, and refined carbohydrates consistently improves outcomes.

### Physical Activity

Exercise improves insulin sensitivity independent of weight loss. Both aerobic exercise and resistance training have independent benefits — combining them is more effective than either alone. Even breaking up prolonged sitting with short walks has measurable glucose benefits.

### Psychological Wellbeing

Diabetes distress — the emotional burden of living with a chronic condition — affects up to 45% of people with diabetes and is distinct from depression, though both are more common in diabetes than in the general population. Psychological support is an underutilised but evidence-based component of diabetes care.

## 8. Know Your Numbers

If you have diabetes, these are the key metrics to monitor and discuss with your healthcare team:

| Measure | Target (most adults) |
|---|---|
| HbA1c | Below 53 mmol/mol (7%) — individualised |
| Fasting glucose | 4–7 mmol/L |
| Blood pressure | Below 130/80 mmHg |
| LDL cholesterol | Below 1.8 mmol/L (high-risk) |
| eGFR (kidney function) | Monitored annually |
| Urine albumin-creatinine ratio | Monitored annually |

Annual screening for retinopathy, neuropathy, and foot health should be part of every person's diabetes care plan.
